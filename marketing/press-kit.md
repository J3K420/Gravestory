## What makes it different

1. **It writes the life story from the scan.** Several apps scan gravestones with AI — the scanning is not the new part. As far as we know, GraveStory is the only one that turns the scan into a written, source-cited life story of any ordinary person. The others stop at transcription and interpretation.
2. **Open sources, cited on every story that draws on them.** The records behind each biography are free, public, and named — WikiTree, Wikidata, Chronicling America's historic newspapers, Internet Archive county histories, Wikipedia — with citations a reader can actually follow, rather than a walled subscription database.
3. **A public community map.** Stories that visitors choose to share appear on a free web map anyone can browse — no account, no app install. The one rival with a comparable map keeps it private by default.
4. **No subscription, ever.** Three free scans, then one-time credit packs from $1.99 that never expire. Rivals in this space are subscription-gated.

## The honest limits

We would rather show you the seams than have you find them.

- **Not every stone yields a rich biography.** A weathered stone, or a person who left no public record, gets a short story built only from what the inscription itself says. That is deliberate: the app never pads a thin record with invented detail, and we never promise depth it can't source.
- **Android only, today.** iOS is planned but not built. A solo developer ships one platform well before starting the second.
- **The stories are AI-assisted compilations of public records — and they can contain errors.** They are not official or authoritative records. That is exactly why every researched fact is cited to a named source a reader can check, why thin records produce short stories instead of confident ones, and why every story carries a report button.

We think honesty about these limits is a feature. An app that admits what it doesn't know is one you can trust when it tells you what it does.

## FAQ — the hard questions

**How accurate are the stories? What happens when one is wrong?**
They are compilations of public records, assembled with AI assistance, and they can contain errors — we say so in the app and on the Play listing. Every fact drawn from a record is cited to a named public source so a reader can check it, and every story has an in-app report button. When the records are thin, the app writes a short story from the inscription alone rather than guessing.

**What about living people?**
The stories are about the deceased, and living relatives' names are redacted before any story becomes public on the community map. Portraits appear only for famous figures, pulled from Wikipedia/Wikidata — the app never surfaces photos of ordinary deceased people, let alone living ones. Anything that slips past those guards can be flagged with the report button.

**What exactly does the AI do, versus the records?**
The AI does two jobs: it reads the name, dates, and inscription off the photograph, and it drafts the narrative. The facts come from the records — WikiTree, Wikidata, historic newspapers, county histories, Wikipedia — searched in parallel and cited in the story. When no records are found, the AI is not allowed to fill the gap; it writes only from what the stone says.

**What happens when there are no records at all?**
You get a short, honest story built from the inscription itself — the name, the dates, whatever the stone reveals — and nothing more. No invented anecdotes, no padded paragraphs. Not every stone yields a rich biography, and the app never pretends otherwise.

**Why credit packs instead of a subscription?**
Because most people visit cemeteries a few times a year, and a subscription would mostly bill them for the months in between. GraveStory sells one-time packs from $1.99 that never expire — after three free scans to see whether it's for you. There is no subscription tier at any price.

**Who owns the photos and stories?**
The photos are yours, and they stay private unless you choose to make a story public — nothing is shared by default. The same goes for the stories: GraveStory claims no ownership of them, and you can make a shared story private again, or delete it, at any time. Public stories appear on the community map, with living relatives' names redacted first. GPS coordinates are used to pin your stories to your map — and to place shared stories on the community map — and are never sold or shared with advertisers.

**How does the community map avoid duplicate graves?**
When two visitors scan the same stone, the app matches on location (within roughly 20 meters) and the name on the stone, and treats them as the same grave — one pin, not one per scan. Approximate locations are marked as approximate on the map rather than presented as exact.

**When is iOS coming?**
It's planned, but not built, and we won't pretend otherwise. GraveStory is a solo project, and the Android app came first; iOS follows once Android has proven itself. Today the honest answer is: Android now, iOS later.

**What data do you collect?**
Your sign-in (email and a display name — the display name appears publicly if you share a story), the photos you take, GPS coordinates to place stories on your map, your purchase history (to grant scan credits), an anonymous device fingerprint (a hash of device brand, model, and OS version, to deter abuse), and basic first-party usage analytics. No ad networks, no third-party analytics SDKs, and nothing is ever sold. Photos are only shared if you make a story public. The full policy is at https://j3k420.github.io/Gravestory/privacy-policy/ and you can delete your account and its data from inside the app.
